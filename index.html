Scraping the National UFO Reporting Center Database

From crash landings in Roswell, to Hollywood blockbusters and the COVID-19 pandemic, spikes in UFO sightings align with current events.

The first recorded UFO, according to the National UFO Reporting Center, was in June 1400. Throughout the next 400 years, rare sightings were recorded in journals, diaries, and official records with feathered pens, fountain pens, and typewriters. Fast forward to the summer of 1947 when a total of 41 UFOs were recorded by citizens staring into the heavens, and we enter the Beige Age of UFOs. Did you know? Scientists have determined the color of space is beige.

In 1947, a top secret crash landing of a U.S. Army weather balloon propelled the United States into a UFO frenzy following a press release and eyewitness reports of a flying disc in Roswell, NM. The "flying disc craze" officially hit the American public and copycat reports of flying saucers flew in by the hundreds. Over the subsequent years, UFO sightings spiked with current events. In 1995 - 1996, the movie releases of "Apollo 13" and "Independance Day" caused UFO sightings to skyrocket going from around 3,000 per decade in the 1970s and 1980s to over 11,000 in the 1990s.



The movie "Apollo 13" was released in 1995, making a gross profit of $172,036,360. Just one year later, "Independence Day" was released bringing in $817.4 million. Sighting counts climbed steadily until they peaked in 2012 - 2015 when the average sightings were sitting at just under 8,000/year. Numbers didn’t reach that height again until 2020 when the pandemic gave people plenty of time to stare out windows.

When that first flying disc landed in Roswell, NM in 1947, it was only natural to send it for further analysis to the Birthplace of Aviation. The flying disc was sent by special plane from New Mexico to Dayton, OH. Only 45 years after the Wright Brothers first flew, Wright Field now analyzed the flight of an entirely different type of aircraft.

While many of us may laugh as we think of our tax dollars going to the study of UFOs, the government took the incident quite seriously. 1947 wasn't the only year Wright Field (today known as Wright Patterson AFB) would look into extraterrestrial activity. Project Blue Book was carried out from 1947 - 1969 at Wright Patterson Air Force Base. It concluded that:

  1. No UFO reported, investigated and evaluated by the Air Force was ever an indication of threat to our national security
  2. There was no evidence submitted to or discovered by the Air Force that sightings categorized as "unidentified" represented technological developments or principles beyond the range of modern scientific knowledge
  3. There was no evidence indicating that sightings categorized as "unidentified" were extraterrestrial vehicles

Despite those findings, citizens around the world continued to record UFO sightings. The bulk of sightings have been within North America, with Canada recording 5,232 sightings and the United States recording 128,127, but over 11,000 sightings have been recorded outside the United States.

After that first famous sighting in Roswell, less than 2,000 UFOs have been seen in New Mexico. In fact, the entire southwest region of the United States has the lowest sighting numbers of the country. Just a bit north, however, and the story changes. California comes in with a whopping 16,000 sightings, almost double any other state. Could it be that aliens like our movies too (watch out, Captain Marvel)?
